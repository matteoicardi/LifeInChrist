# Desire and the Ache of the Heart (Marriage Preparation Notes)

These notes are for engaged couples preparing for Christian marriage. They aim to help you speak honestly about desire (including sexual desire), to pray without masks, and to understand marriage as a sacrament that points beyond itself to the Bridegroom who alone satisfies.

## 1) Start with reality: desire is not the enemy

Many people approach marriage prep with hidden fears:

- “My desires are too much.”
- “If I’m honest, I’ll be judged.”
- “Holiness means shrinking my heart.”

The Christian claim is the opposite: desire is a clue. It reveals that you were made for communion and joy *stronger than death*.

Jesus’ first question in John’s Gospel is not “Are you behaving?” but:

> “What do you seek?” (John 1:38)

Marriage prep begins well when a couple can name what they seek: love, safety, beauty, joy, belonging, intimacy, fruitfulness, a home.

## 2) The “cemeteries of desire”: learning to pray without a mask

Most of us carry places in the heart where we buried unfulfilled desires, disappointments, rejections, and losses. We often “manage” them, spiritualise them away, or cover them with performance.

One simple “rule” for prayer that belongs in marriage prep is:

> “Whatever gets stirred up, do not censor it.  
> Do not spiritualise it away.  
> Just feel how much you wanted it.”

A simple, powerful way to pray is to return to these memories with Jesus and refuse to censor:

- grief (“I really wanted this”)
- anger (“Why did you allow this?”)
- shame (“I feel foolish for wanting it”)
- fear (“If I hope again, I’ll be crushed”)

Concrete examples (because couples need to practice on real stories):

- A childhood joy that was taken away (“I finally got it… and then it was gone.”)
- A romantic “yes” that turned into rejection (“I felt seen… then I felt ignored.”)

This is not irreverence. Even the cry “Why have you abandoned me?” is a prayer Jesus prays on the Cross (Psalm 22; Matthew 27:46). God is not scandalised by honest desire. He already knows your heart.

This kind of honesty can feel “too much” or “sinful,” but the deeper danger in prayer is not honest emotion; it is hiding behind religious masks—performing for God instead of meeting Him.

Sometimes the turning point is realising: *my problem is not that I have a hungry heart; my problem is that I keep trying to look “holy” instead of letting God meet me in the truth.* Christ sees what is hidden and is not scandalised by it (John 1:48).

The Catechism gives a key that belongs in marriage prep:

> “Prayer is the encounter of God’s thirst with ours.” (CCC 2560)

So prayer is not first a performance for God; it is a meeting of desires: God’s desire for you, and your desire for life.

## 3) Why created goods don’t “hold”: finite things can’t bear infinite desire

A created good can be truly good—friendship, romance, beauty, sex, a home, a child—but it cannot carry the weight of being “God” for you.

When we demand infinite fulfillment from a finite thing, two things happen:

1. The good thing gets crushed by impossible expectations.
2. We get trapped in the cycle of “more”: more reassurance, more pleasure, more control, more proof.

Augustine named it:

> “Our hearts are restless until they rest in you.”

This is not pessimism about creation. It is the *right size* of the human heart.

God is not a cosmic killjoy. Holiness is not emotional numbness or pretending we don’t want things. The point is not to destroy pleasure, but to save it from being crushed under impossible expectations.

## 4) Desire in marriage: icon, not idol

Christian marriage is a sacrament: it makes visible an invisible mystery. That means marital love is meant to be an icon—something that truly reveals God—without becoming an idol.

Two common distortions:

- **Idolatry:** “My spouse must complete me, heal me, guarantee my happiness, remove my loneliness.”
- **Iconoclasm:** “Desire is dangerous; shut it down; don’t want too much; numb the heart.”

The Christian path is redemption: desire healed and aimed toward communion.

## 5) Chastity: a “yes” to more, not less

Chastity is not hatred of the body and not fear of pleasure. It is the refusal to short-circuit infinite desire with temporary substitutes.

The Catechism’s frame is integration and freedom:

- Chastity is the successful integration of sexuality within the person (CCC 2337).
- It “gathers” us back into unity (CCC 2340).
  - (For a fuller read: CCC 2337–2345; on purity of heart: CCC 2517–2520.)

Practically, chastity means:

- learning to love the person, not consume the person
- learning to wait, communicate, and sacrifice
- learning to bring desire into the light instead of acting it out in secret

Before marriage, this protects the couple from building the relationship on gratification. Within marriage, it protects love from turning into entitlement.

### Chastity is a matter of the heart (not a “columns chart”)

It’s tempting to reduce purity to a list:

- “Allowed before marriage” (Column A)
- “Not allowed before marriage” (Column B)

Rules matter, but they are not the whole story. Two people can “technically behave” and still be using each other. Jesus goes straight to the heart (Matthew 5:27–28), because God looks at the heart (1 Samuel 16:7).

The deepest marriage‑prep question is not only *“What are we doing?”* but:

**“Why am I doing it—am I loving, or am I grasping?”**

Here is a strong (and often misunderstood) summary from the tradition:

> “Love, and do what you will.” (St. Augustine, *In Epistulam Ioannis ad Parthos Tractatus* 7.8)

This is not permission to do anything. It means: if charity truly governs you, you will not will what destroys the other. St Paul says the same truth in another way:

- “Love does no wrong to a neighbor; therefore love is the fulfilling of the law.” (Romans 13:10)
- “The whole law is fulfilled in one word: ‘You shall love your neighbor as yourself.’” (Galatians 5:14)

For engaged couples, chastity is the school where desire learns to become love.

## 6) Sexual desire: fascination can go to heaven or hell

The human heart is fascinated by the mystery of male and female. That fascination is not automatically “bad.”

But fascination must be **directed**:

- toward communion and self-gift (heavenward)
- or toward grasping and consumption (hellward)

Original sin tends to invert desire: what was meant to launch us toward communion becomes fuel for self-destruction. The Christian claim is not “you are disgusting,” but “you are made for more”—and Christ comes to heal and redirect what is disordered.

In marriage prep, avoid reducing purity to a “permitted behaviors chart.” You can “technically behave” while still using the other person. The deeper question is: **what is my heart seeking—and is it love or self?**

Pope Benedict XVI gives a helpful high-level lens: love needs purification and healing so that eros becomes truly human and truly open to God (*Deus Caritas Est*).

St. John Paul II’s Theology of the Body is the same project in a different voice: not shame-based suppression, but redemption—learning to see and live desire as a call to communion.

## 7) The spousal meaning: covenant love, not a contract

Scripture gives the key for the meaning of marriage:

> “This is a great mystery… in reference to Christ and the Church.” (Ephesians 5:31–32)

Marriage is not mainly a contract for mutual benefit; it is a covenant—a vowed gift of self.

This matters for desire: covenant love does not treat the other as a tool for my needs; it learns the freedom of self-gift.

### The “balance of the gift” (a concrete way to examine love)

In real marital love, giving and receiving are meant to interpenetrate:

- I give in a way that *welcomes* the other (not forcing, not taking).
- I receive in a way that *gives* myself (not using, not manipulating).

This is one of the simplest marriage‑prep questions you can ask in moments of tension or intimacy:

**Am I trying to restore the balance of the gift—or am I trying to get my own way?**

## 8) A couple’s practice: desire → truth → prayer → gift

Use this as a simple weekly exercise (20–30 minutes):

1. **Name one desire** you felt strongly this week (emotional, relational, sexual, spiritual).
2. **Name the fear under it:** fear of rejection, not enough, being controlled, being alone.
3. **Name the temptation:** to grasp, to test, to withdraw, to escape into fantasy, to control.
4. **Speak one truth:** “You are not my savior, but you are my gift.” / “I won’t punish you for being honest.” / “We can bring this into the light.”
5. **Pray one sentence each** (out loud, simple): “Jesus, meet me here.” / “Jesus, teach me to love.” / “Holy Spirit, heal our desire.”

## 9) Questions for engaged couples (for conversation, not debate)

- Where do I tend to demand that my spouse “be God for me” (security, worth, identity)?
- What are my “cemeteries of desire” that I’m afraid to revisit with Jesus?
- When do I hide behind a religious mask (performance) instead of praying honestly?
- What helps me feel safe enough to tell the truth about my heart and body?
- What patterns do we bring into conflict: grasping, controlling, withdrawing, pleasing?
- What would it look like to move from “getting” to “giving” in our relationship this week?

## 10) A final kerygmatic reminder for marriage prep

Your spouse is not the source of your worth. Your worth is given.

Marriage is real, beautiful, and demanding—but it is not “the end.” It points beyond itself to the wedding feast that will never take its “yes” back:

> “Blessed are those who are invited to the marriage supper of the Lamb.” (Revelation 19:9)

If marriage prep becomes moralism, it will crush you. If marriage prep becomes honest prayer and redeemed desire, it will prepare you for a covenant that can last.

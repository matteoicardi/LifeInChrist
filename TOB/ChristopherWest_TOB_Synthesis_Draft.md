# Theology of the Body: Desire, Beauty, and Interior Integration  
**A Synthesis of Reflections by Dr. Christopher West**

---

## Part I: The Ache of the Human Heart

### The Creative Restlessness: Beauty That Wounds

In the opening pages of *Eating the Sunrise*, I describe an annual sadness that comes upon me in early May. A week earlier, the redbud trees were in full bloom; now their petals carpet the ground. The fleetingness of their beauty puts a pit in my stomach.

This reaction feels "disproportionate" until we recognise what beauty does: it awakens desire. Created beauty stirs the heart and, precisely because it is finite, it cannot bear the weight of what it awakens.

- Beauty appears -> desire ignites.
- Beauty fades -> the ache is exposed.
- The pattern repeats -> and underneath it all stands the same fact: death.

Pope Benedict XVI frames this as the fundamental human problem: the heart cries out for a joy, love, and beauty *stronger than death*, something that does not come and go.

This is not sentimentality; it is anthropology. The ache is evidence that we were made for eternity.

### Homesickness and the Gospel's First Question

G.K. Chesterton observes that we can feel homesick even when we're at home. That homesickness is the signature of a creature made for more than the created order can supply.

Jesus does not begin by giving a list of duties. In John's Gospel, his first words are a question aimed straight at desire:

> "What do you seek?" (John 1:38)

St Augustine teaches that the whole Christian life is a journey into longing. If the hunger for infinite joy, infinite love, and infinite beauty is not awakened, Christianity collapses into moralism—rule-keeping without fire.

Augustine's diagnosis remains undefeated:

> "You have made us for yourself, O Lord, and our hearts are restless until they rest in you."

Ethics, then, is not the suppression of desire. It is the schooling of desire—aiming it toward what it was made for.

### Evangelisation as "Awakening the Ache" (Springsteen and the "O")

One of the sharpest lines in this whole arc of West's teaching is simple: **evangelisation is the awakening of the ache.**

West tells the story of being eight years old in the 1970s, lying in bed, when Bruce Springsteen's *Born to Run* came on the radio. The song is charged with unquenchable thirst. At the end, Springsteen cracks open his ribcage and lets out a guttural, non-verbal cry—an extended **"Oh!"**

West calls it an "O Antiphon." The Church's O Antiphons are ancient Advent cries of longing—*O Sapientia, O Adonai, O Emmanuel*—the soul's plea for the Messiah.

St John of the Cross says that "Oh!" is the expression of profound longing for the infinite. When West heard that cry, he knew: whatever Springsteen wanted, he wanted too.

The tragedy, West admits, is that his religious formation did not connect the dots. No one said: "That ache is a clue. That ache is prayer in seed form. Heaven is wooing you."

Pope Benedict XVI gives the key:

> "Music uncovers the buried way to the heart. And wherever this happens, music becomes the road that leads us to Jesus."

Prayer becomes, in this light, an exercise of desire: learning to aim the awakened ache toward the true banquet.

### Two Bad Options and the One Christian Alternative

When desire awakens, people are usually offered only two paths:

1. Indulgence (the "fast food" gospel): take your thirst to finite pleasures.
2. Repression (the "starvation diet" gospel): treat desire itself as the enemy.

If those are the only choices, many will choose the chicken nuggets. But the Gospel offers a third way: **redemption**—desire redirected, purified, and fulfilled.

This is the heartbeat of West's approach across these three conversations: do not numb the ache; do not idolise the ache's objects; **let the ache become a prayer** that opens onto Christ.

### Prayer Without a Mask: The "Cemeteries of Desire"

West offers a concrete way to understand what it means to "let the ache become prayer": return, in God's presence, to what he calls the *cemeteries of desire*—the places in the heart where we have buried unfulfilled longings and disappointments.

The temptation in religious settings is to keep prayer "clean": to censor anger, grief, and hunger so that we can present a polite, controlled self to God. West's point is that this is not holiness; it is a mask.

In one retreat experience, he describes finally letting the buried ache surface—anger included—and discovering that the real sin was not honesty, but performance. What changed was not that God became safer, but that West stopped trying to impress God.

Theologically, this connects to three key anchors:

- **Union with Christ in the cry of abandonment:** When the heart prays "Why?" in truth, it can be praying in union with Jesus' own prayer on the Cross (Psalm 22; Matthew 27:46).
- **The Lord who sees the hidden seeker:** West ties this to Nathanael's fig-tree moment—Christ sees our private seeking and is not scandalised by it (John 1:48).
- **Prayer as desire meeting desire:** The Catechism frames prayer not as technique but as encounter: “Prayer is the encounter of God’s thirst with ours” (CCC 2560).

Practically, this section strengthens the whole project: chastity, healing, and evangelisation all depend on learning to bring the real hunger into the light—without numbing it, and without making it an idol.

## Part II: Eros and the Rocket Engines

### *Eros*: The "Holy Passion" That Seeks God

The Church is not embarrassed to use the Greek word *eros* to name the ache for God. That shocks modern ears because "erotic" has been flattened into "pornographic." West's point is sharper: we have confused *eros* with *porneia*.

- *Eros*: the upward impulse of the human spirit toward everything true, good, and beautiful—ultimately toward God.
- *Porneia*: the twisting, distortion, and reduction of that desire—desire turned into consumption.

Benedict XVI states it baldly in *Deus Caritas Est*:

> "Eros is the desire in us that seeks God."

Christianity does not aim to kill *eros*. It aims to free it, heal it, and raise it to its true height.

### The Rocket-Engine Analogy (and Why Repression Is Not Holiness)

West's rocket-engine analogy is deliberately visceral: God gave *eros* as the fuel of a rocket designed to launch us to the stars—to the infinite. But the enemy does not want us to reach the stars, so he **inverts the engines**. The result is predictable: crash and burn.

This immediately exposes the lie behind the "starvation diet" approach: repression is not sanctity. If you treat passion itself as evil, you drift into the Manichean error ("spirit good, body bad"). West repeats a principle of Catholic cosmology that demolishes that error:

> The devil doesn't have his own clay.

Everything God creates is good. Evil is parasitic. It only twists what is already good.

### The Three Choices of *Eros*: Fast Food, Starvation, Banquet

Across the podcasts, West keeps returning to a triad (famously illustrated by his daughter's drawing):

1. Indulgence — aim infinite hunger at finite pleasure.
2. Repression — condemn the hunger itself.
3. Redemption — open the hunger to purification so it can be redirected toward God.

Both indulgence and repression are "half right." Indulgence at least admits the desire is real. Repression at least admits we cannot trust disordered desire. But both miss the Gospel's centre: Christ comes to **redeem desire**, not amputate it.

### Addiction as a Theological Category

West proposes a blunt theological definition:

> Addiction happens when we aim our desire for infinite joy at a finite pleasure.

Finite pleasures can be genuine goods; they can even be foretastes of heaven. But when we mistake the appetiser for the banquet, we lock ourselves into the cycle of "more."

This also explains a startling evangelical fact: sinners and "messy" people often run to Jesus faster than the morally comfortable. West echoes the line attributed to Augustine: those lost in their passions can be less lost than those who have lost their passions—because at least the hunger is still alive.

The greatest danger is not disordered desire; it is **dead desire**.

### The Crumpled Painting: Manichaeism vs Redemption

West's crumpled-painting image clarifies what redemption actually is. Imagine a painting of man and woman "naked without shame" (Genesis 2:25): an icon of God's inner life. The enemy crumples the painting.

- Manichaeism looks at the crumpled painting and says: "Trash. Throw it away."
- Redemption says: "Do not throw it away. Let Christ uncrumple it."

This is why purification often hurts: uncrumpling is not gentle. But it is mercy.

And this is where West begins to connect theology to therapy: sometimes it is not "me" who is heretical, but a wounded *part* of me that has believed a lie about God, the body, or desire.

Redemption is not mere willpower. It is allowing grace to reach the places in us that are bent.

## Part III: The Great Mystery (Spousal Meaning of the Body)

### The Woman at the Well

The story of the Woman at the Well (John 4) illustrates this redirection. She says, "I have no husband." Jesus replies, "You have had five husbands, and the one you have now is not your husband."

In biblical numerology, six is the number of imperfection. She has tried to satisfy her thirst with six "imperfect lovers." Who is her seventh lover? Seven is the number of perfection. Her seventh lover is **Christ the Bridegroom** standing right in front of her.

The well is the traditional place of courtship in the Old Testament. Jesus meets her there to show that He is the true Bridegroom who offers the "living water" that finally satisfies.

### The Spousal Meaning of the Body

The body is not just a biological shell; it is a theology. *St. Paul* sums this up in Ephesians 5:31-32:

> "For this reason a man shall leave his father and mother and be joined to his wife, and the two shall become one flesh. This is a great mystery, and I mean in reference to Christ and the church."

The holy communion of man and woman is a sign in this world of the holy communion consummated in the Eucharist.

- The Trinity: God is an eternal exchange of life-giving love.
- The Image: "Male and female he created them" (Genesis 1:27). Our bodies make visible the invisible mystery of God.
- The Call: We are called to communion. A man's body makes no sense without a woman's, and vice versa. Together they reveal the call to life-giving love.

### The Cross as the Marriage Bed

St. John intimately links the wedding feast at Cana with the Cross. At Cana, Jesus says, "My hour has not yet come." What hour? The hour of the Cross.

St. Augustine calls the Cross the **"marriage bed"** where the marriage of Christ and the Church is forever consummated.

- The New Adam (Christ) says to the New Eve (the Church/Mary): "This is my body given for you."
- The blood and water flowing from His side are the spiritual seed that brings new life.

Therefore, the *Catechism (CCC 1617)* states:

> "The entire Christian life bears the mark of the spousal love of Christ and the Church."

## Part IV: Liturgy, Beauty, and "Eating the Sunrise"

### Liturgy as Orientation

Liturgy is the orientation of our desire toward the coming of the Bridegroom. Traditionally, the Church prayed *ad orientem* (toward the East), symbolizing the Church (Bride) turning to face the rising sun (Christ/Bridegroom).

Psalm 19 says, "The sun comes forth like a bridegroom from his tent."

When we go to Mass, we are there to **"Eat the Sunrise."** We take into our bodies the true Bridegroom. The Eucharist is the sacrament of the Bridegroom and the Bride.

### Beauty and Theophany

All of creation is a **Theophany** (a showing of God). A redbud tree, a sunrise, or a starry night—these are all icons or windows into Heaven.

- Iconography: Created things reveal the Creator.
- Idolatry: Stopping at the icon and worshipping it instead of what it points to.
- Iconoclasm: Destroying the icon because we fear idolatry.

The Christian way is the middle path: recognizing the **Sacramentality** of creation. We don't just want to *see* beauty; as C.S. Lewis says, we want to bathe in it, to become part of it. That is what happens in the Eucharist.

### The Scandal of the Particular (The "*Hic*")

God's love is not abstract; it is particular. The Latin word *hic* means "here." The Word was made flesh *here*—in a specific place, time, and body.

- Why did Christ die on a cross?
- Why at that specific time?

A student once told a priest friend: "To save the good thief."

God becomes particular so we can relate to Him. The Crucifix shows us a God who knows *our* specific pain.

## Part V: Interior Integration and Healing (IFS)

### The "Inner Man" and Parts

We often feel fractured. St. John Paul II speaks of the "Inner Man." In the context of **Internal Family Systems (IFS)**, this aligns with the concept of the **Self** (the *Imago Dei* within us) and our various **Parts**.

- We have parts that are **Managers** (Pharisees) who try to earn love by following rules.
- We have parts that are **Firefighters** (Addicts) who try to numb pain with "fast food" pleasures.
- We have **Exiles** (Wounded Children) who carry the burden of shame and unlovability.

### Tenderness as the Key

John Paul II uses the word **tenderness**. A husband must show tenderness to his wife to help her feel safe. Similarly, our "Inmost Self" (in union with the Holy Spirit) must show tenderness to our wounded parts.

- If we treat our parts like "Hector the Marital Debt Collector" (demanding they shape up), they will shut down.
- If we listen to them with compassion, they can be unburdened and integrated.

### Chastity as Integration

The *Catechism (CCC 2340)* states:

> "It is through chastity that we are gathered together and led back to the unity from which we were fragmented into multiplicity."

- Lust fractures us.
- Chastity reintegrates us.

This is not just about avoiding bad behaviors; it is about the **reintegration** of our internal family.

## Part VI: Practical Application - Staying in the Ache

### The One Temptation

Monsignor Lorenzo Albacete said every temptation comes down to one: **The temptation to believe that the satisfaction of the deepest desires of my heart is totally up to me.**

This is "doubting the Gift." When we doubt God will satisfy us, we grasp at idols.

### The Solution: Psalm 119:11

When tempted to grasp at the "Fast Food," I use this scripture:

> "I treasure your promise in my heart, O Lord, lest I sin against you."

What is the promise? "Come to me, all you who are hungry, and I will satisfy you."

### Conclusion: Ordinary Mysticism

We are all called to be mystics—not necessarily levitating, but living the "ordinary mysticism" of opening our hearts to God.

We must learn to **"Stay in the Ache."** Do not numb it (repression) and do not stuff it with false idols (indulgence). Let the ache stand as a prayer, a hunger that only the Bridegroom can fill.

> "Don't be afraid when I die. Don't be afraid to die with me. Look, I revive." — Poem by John Paul II
